## Part 1 — Bean Rest & Freshness

### Why Beans Need to Rest

After roasting, beans release CO₂ through a process called degassing. Brewing too early means excess CO₂ disrupts extraction — in espresso it causes instability, uneven flow, and sour/harsh notes; in filter it produces a sour, grassy, underdeveloped cup. Brewing too late means oxidation has degraded the aromatic compounds that make specialty coffee worth drinking.

**Three critical facts:**
1. Light roasts degas ~3× slower than dark roasts (denser cellular structure traps CO₂ longer)
2. Natural and honey process coffees need 3–7 extra days vs. washed (denser, more complex bean structure)
3. Espresso requires more rest than filter — pressurized extraction amplifies CO₂ harshness significantly

### Stage Definitions

| Stage | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **Too Fresh** | Heavy CO₂ degassing in progress. Espresso: excessive crema, instability, sour/harsh. Filter: sour, grassy, underdeveloped. Do not brew. |
| **Early Peak** | Acceptable but not optimal. CO₂ still muting clarity. Some brightness or harshness present. |
| **Peak** | Sweet spot. CO₂ adequately released, aromatic compounds not yet oxidized. Balanced extraction, sweetness, clarity. |
| **Late Peak** | Still very good. Minor aromatic loss beginning. Brightness may shift to chocolate/caramel notes. |
| **Aging** | Noticeably past peak. Oxidation visible in cup. Still drinkable but use soon. |
| **Old** | Not recommended. Significant flavor loss, rancidity risk (especially dark roasts), oxidized oils. |

---

### Special Warnings

- **Dark roasts go rancid fast** — Scott Rao: dark roasts taste rancid if you wait past ~1–2 days *for cupping assessment*, but for consumer brewing, 3–7 days is the peak window. Beyond 16 days, expect significant degradation.
- **SCA 8–24 hour standard is misleading** — that's the cupping/assessment protocol, not consumer guidance. It's intentionally early to assess the roast character before peak consumer flavor develops.
- **Grinding accelerates staling dramatically** — 84% of aromatic volatiles are lost within 60 minutes of grinding (JayArr Coffee, aroma staling kinetics research). Always grind immediately before brewing.

## Part 2 — Brew Method Suitability by Bean

Light roasts are very challenging for espresso — high acidity under pressure is difficult to balance. Dark/natural is the classic espresso combination.

### Origin Modifiers (applied on top of base score)

| Origin Group | Espresso | V60 | AeroPress |
|-------------|----------|-----|-----------|
| East Africa (Ethiopia, Kenya, Rwanda, Burundi, Uganda, Tanzania) | −6 | +8 | +5 |
| Brazil | +8 | −5 | +4 |
| Indonesia (Sumatra, Java, Sulawesi, Flores, Papua) | +6 | −8 | +4 |
| Central/South America (Colombia, Guatemala, Costa Rica, Honduras, El Salvador, Nicaragua, Mexico, Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador) | +3 | +3 | +2 |
| Yemen | −3 | +6 | +5 |

**Rationale:**
- East African coffees have intense natural acidity — this amplifies unpleasantly under espresso pressure but shines in filter brewing
- Brazilian coffees are low-acid, body-forward — ideal espresso base beans, less interesting on V60
- Indonesian coffees are earthy and full-bodied — great espresso body, but V60 turns them muddy
- Central/South American coffees are balanced and versatile — small positive across the board
- Yemen is complex and wine-like — challenging for espresso balance, very interesting on filter

---

### Origin-Specific Brew Notes

#### Espresso
- **East Africa** — High natural acidity under pressure. Use higher ratio (2.5–3×) to avoid sharpness.
- **Brazil** — Low-acid, body-forward. Ideal for espresso. Lower ratios (1.8–2×) enhance sweetness.
- **Indonesia** — Earthy and full-bodied. Keep ratio tight (1.8–2×).

#### V60
- **East Africa** — Beans shine on V60. Expect bright citrus, floral, and stone fruit notes.
- **Brazil** — Produces a mild, nutty cup. AeroPress may better highlight body and sweetness.
- **Indonesia** — Earthy and heavy. AeroPress is a better fit.

#### AeroPress
- **Brazil** — Body and chocolate notes come through nicely.
- **East Africa** — Clean, bright cup. Steep on the shorter end.

## Part 4 — Extraction Diagnosis

### Espresso Diagnosis

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Sour (no bitter) | Under-extracted | Grind finer, increase dose |
| Bitter (no sour) | Over-extracted | Grind coarser, reduce dose |
| Harsh | Brew temp too high | Lower temperature |
| Watery | Ratio too high or dose too low | Increase dose or reduce yield |
| Choked flow | Grind too fine | Go coarser in small steps |
| Fast flow | Grind too coarse | Go finer in small steps |
| Wet puck | Channelling | Improve distribution and tamp |
| Shot too fast (<20s) | Grind too coarse | Finer grind |
| Shot too slow (>40s) | Grind too fine | Coarser grind |

### Filter Diagnosis (V60 / AeroPress)

| Symptom | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Sour | Under-extracted | Grind finer, increase steep time |
| Bitter | Over-extracted | Grind coarser, reduce steep time |
| Watery / flat | Ratio too weak or dose too low | More coffee or less water |
| Too fast (<2min V60) | Grind too coarse | Finer grind |
| Too slow (>4min V60) | Grind too fine | Coarser grind |

## Part 5 — Stock Management

### Remaining Grams Calculation

If a bag has a manual `remainingGrams` override, use that directly.

Otherwise: `remaining = purchasedGrams − (sum of all brews × 1.12)`

The 1.12 factor accounts for ~12% waste (clinging grounds, retained liquid, spillage).

### Depletion Thresholds

- **< 15g remaining** → effectively empty (not enough for a standard dose)
- **> 40% of purchase** → green indicator
- **15–40% of purchase** → amber indicator
- **< 15% of purchase** → red indicator
